4.0 Standards, Practices, Conventions, and Metrics

4.1 Purpose

To measure anything, you need an approved standard to which the item is compared. Quality is no different. The purpose of section 4 in the SQAP is to describe those standards, practices, conventions, and metrics that will be used to measure your product's quality. That way you can see if the product satisfies its quality objectives, or if more work needs to be done.
--JC.

4.2 Content

Here is where you provide the complete list. For each list item, state how compliance will be monitored and assured.

For example, you may need to say that your Documentation Standards are as follows:... and then actually describe the standards (e.g. all documentation needs to be spell checked, grammar checked, conforms to HTML level 4.0, all the hyper links need to be valid, etc.).

You will also need some Coding Standards. I suggest that you look at the Code Complete textbook to get good ideas for this. Consider such things as: a standard indentation level of 3 spaces; comments written at the level of intent (the why, not the how); each file shall have a header block that lists the name, purpose, and modification history of the file; the use and creation of global data discouraged; variable names need to accurately and completely describe the purpose; what is the recommended length of a routine?; what is the recommended length of a routine name?; what is the recommended length of a variable name?; what is the recommended length of a file?; what is the recommended length of a file name?; what is the recommended length of a source code line?; dicuss coupling and cohesion; etc.

You will also need to discuss your Testing Standards. Define and address the importance of things like unit testing, module testing, path testing, integration testing, system testing, qualification testing, etc. When will you do these? Will you do all of them? How will you know if they get done or not? Be sure that what you write in this section answers such questions.

One of the metrics you may use is the number of items reported at the inspection meetings. Another metric might be the total amount of time (for all reviewers) that was spent reviewing the product. When you enter the incremental build cycle next quarter, you may use PPRs as a form of metrics. Try to think about other metrics that you may need to collect that help measure and assess the quality of the products.
--JC.
